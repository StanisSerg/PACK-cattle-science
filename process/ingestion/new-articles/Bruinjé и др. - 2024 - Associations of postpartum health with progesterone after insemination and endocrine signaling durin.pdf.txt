ABSTRACT
Our objective was to investigate associations of 
postpartum health with serum progesterone (P4) con-
centrations after first artificial insemination (AI), ex-pression of interferon-stimulated genes (ISG) in blood leukocytes, and serum pregnancy-associated glycopro-tein (PAG) during early pregnancy in dairy cows. In this observational study, Holstein cows (n = 475) from 2 commercial herds were enrolled 3 wk before expected parturition. Body condition score (BCS) and lameness were assessed at enrollment, and total Ca, haptoglobin, and nonesterified fatty acids (NEFA) were measured in serum at 2 and 6 ± 2 DIM. Blood β-hydroxybutyrate and metritis were evaluated at 4, 8, 11, and 15 ± 2 DIM, and purulent vaginal discharge (PVD) and endometri-tis based on endometrial cytology were diagnosed at 35 DIM. Onset of cyclicity was assessed by biweekly P4 measurements. BCS was assessed at enrollment and at 63 DIM, and lameness at enrollment and at 21 and 49 DIM. First AI was based on estrus detection until ~75 DIM, or synchronization for timed AI thereafter. Serum P4 was measured at d 8 and 12 after first AI, expres-sion of ISG (ISG15 and RTP4) in blood leukocytes was assessed at d 19, and PAG in serum of pregnant cows was measured at d 29, 33, and 40. Multivariable linear regression models were built including health variables (with metabolites categorized by ROC curve analysis) and covariates (season, milk yield, AI method, DIM), accounting for pregnancy status (for ISG and PAG models), repeated measures (for P4 and PAG models), and herd as random. Results are reported comparing cows affected vs. unaffected by each predictor. Serum P4 concentrations were lesser at d 8 after AI in cows that had total Ca ≤2.09 mM (3.6 vs. 4.0 ± 0.2 ng/mL) at 2 DIM or a clinical disease (3.7 vs. 4.0 ± 0.2 mg/mL), and P4 tended to be lesser at d 8 in cows that had PVD (3.6 vs. 4.0 ± 0.2 ng/mL) or any uterine disease (3.7 vs. 4.1 ± 0.2 ng/mL). Among cows pregnant at d 29, relative ISG15 expression (fold change) at d 19 was greater in cows that had lameness [5.10 (4.18 to 6.18) vs. 3.14 (3.10 to 3.19)] but tended to be lesser in cows that had endometritis [3.20 (2.89 to 3.54) vs. 4.29 (4.20 to 4.35)], and relative expression of RTP4 tended to be lesser in cows that had displaced abomasum [1.02 (0.62 to 1.67) vs. 1.75 (1.73 to 1.78)]. Serum PAG was lesser at d 29 [3.8 vs. 4.2 ± 0.2 sample-to-positive ratio (S:P)] in cows that had NEFA ≥0.73 mM at 6 DIM, and lesser at d 33 (3.9 vs. 4.5 ± 0.2 S:P) and 40 (3.1 vs. 3.7 ± 0.2 S:P) in cows that had prepartum BCS ≥3.75. These findings suggest long-term effects of metabolic or reproductive tract disorders on luteal function after first AI, pregnancy recognition signaling, and placental function during early pregnancy. These associations may partially explain some of the pathways linking postpartum health and fertility in dairy cows.Key words: Disease, Fertility, Reproduction, Transition health
INTRODUCTION
Transition dairy cows undergo several physiologi-
cal changes associated with parturition and metabolic adaptations to the onset of lactation. These changes are commonly characterized by decreased feed intake, immune dysfunction, systemic and uterine inflamma-tion, mineral imbalance, and negative energy balance (LeBlanc, 2012; Horst et al., 2021). When excessive or combined, these physiological stressors can become risk factors for metabolic or uterine disorders, observed in nearly half of early postpartum cows (LeBlanc, 2012; Bruinjé et al., 2023b). A key consequence of metabolic and inflammatory diseases is impaired reproductive performance. Severe or prolonged hypocalcemia (Ven-jakob et al., 2018), elevated blood NEFA or hapto-globin (Hp), hyperketonemia (HYK), and uterine or non-uterine clinical diseases have been associated with reduced probability of estrus detection, pregnancy, and increased pregnancy losses (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Bruinjé et al., 2023a; b). of the ovary, uterus, and early conceptus are crucial 
for successful establishment and maintenance of preg-nancy. The increase in circulating P4 after formation of the corpus luteum (CL) is essential to support early embryo survival and development, conceptus elonga-tion, and subsequent attachment (Carter et al., 2008; Forde et al., 2009; Clemente et al., 2009). Suboptimal P4 concentrations before d 7 after AI alter expres-sion of endometrial genes associated with histotroph composition and the uterine environment and impair embryo development by d 14 (Forde et al., 2009, 2011). After d 16, pregnancy recognition (CL maintenance) is signaled by interferon-tau (IFN-τ) produced by the conceptus, which can stimulate the expression of endo-metrial genes associated with amino acid transport in vitro, and is likely associated with an enhanced uter-ine environment (Groebner et al., 2011). In pregnant cows, the binucleate giant cells at the fetal-maternal interface secrete PAG that can be detected in maternal circulation starting at 25 d of gestation (Giordano et al., 2012). Lower circulating pregnancy-specific protein B (PSPB) at 51 d of gestation was observed in high-risk pregnancies (small embryo size, slow heartbeat, or extra-amniotic membrane) compared with normal pregnancies (Peixoto et al., 2021). Lower PAG or PSPB at d 24 or 29 of gestation was reported in cows that lost the pregnancy by d 40 (Bruinjé et al., 2023a) or by d 61 to 74 of gestation (Mercadante et al., 2016; Ribeiro et al., 2014; Stangaferro et al., 2021), and lower PAG was observed in pregnant cows that subsequently had calving problems compared with cows that had normal calving (Mercadante et al., 2016). These studies suggest that lesser PAG level during early gestation is associ-ated with increased risk of unsuccessful pregnancy.
Although the physiological mechanisms above are 
crucial for early pregnancy establishment and mainte-nance, it is unclear if they are influenced by postpartum health. Expression of ISG in blood leukocytes (PBL) at d 20, and concentrations of PSPB from d 25 to 67 were reduced in cows with lesser P4 concentrations from 6 to 11 d after AI (Carvalho et al., 2017). When timed AI was used for first breeding, cows that had postpartum clinical diseases had lesser P4 concentration in plasma, impaired elongation of the preimplantation conceptus, and reduced ISG expression in PBL (Ribeiro et al., 2016). However, little is known about biological mecha-nisms linking postpartum health and metabolism with subsequent fertility in herds using mostly estrus de-tection for first breeding. We hypothesized that cows with postpartum health disorders would have lesser P4 concentrations in serum after first AI, lower relative expression of ISG in PBL, and lesser PAG levels in serum during early pregnancy than unaffected cows. Therefore, our objective was to investigate associations of postpartum health with serum P4 concentrations after AI, expression of ISG in PBL, and serum PAG levels during early pregnancy in lactating Holstein cows inseminated mostly after estrus detection. A secondary objective was to assess relationships among P4 concen-trations, expression of ISG, and PAG levels.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
This study was approved by the Animal Care Com-
mittee at the University of Guelph (AUP #4385) and is reported following the STROBE-Vet checklist guidelines (Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology – Veterinary Extension; O’Connor et al., 2016).
Description of herds
Two commercial Holstein dairy herds in southwest-
ern Ontario were enrolled in this prospective observa-tional study. This study uses a subsample of cows in an observational study of transition period health and estrus detection that enrolled 1,532 prepartum Holstein cows (Bruinjé et al., 2023b) between April 2019 and November 2020. The subsample [137 nulliparous (77 in herd A, 60 in herd B) and 337 parous cows (207 in herd A, 130 in herd B)] were also evaluated in a companion manuscript (Bruinjé et al., 2023a), and was selected based on the weekday that first AI occurred, so sampling days after AI would be within the planned sampling interval described below. Herds were selected purposively, according to their proximity to the Uni-versity of Guelph (Guelph, ON, Canada), their use of automated activity monitors (AAM) as a primary tool for reproductive management for first AI, enrollment in DHIA, established record keeping, and willingness to participate in the study. Descriptive information on the herds was obtained from DairyComp 305 (Valley Ag Software). Herd A milked approximately 470 Holstein cows 3 times a day using a parallel parlor system while herd B milked approximately 430 Holstein cows using an automated milking system with an average of 3.2 milkings per day. Production averaged 38 kg/d (11,558 kg 305 d milk at third DHIA milk test) for primiparous and 47 kg/d (14,256 kg 305 d milk) for multiparous cows in herd A, and 32 kg/d (9,931 kg 305 d milk) for primiparous and 41 kg/d (12,495 kg 305 d milk) for multiparous cows in herd B. During the study, 21-d pregnancy rate, 21-d insemination rate, and pregnancy risk averaged 25%, 62%, and 40% in herd A, and 23%, 63%, and 36% in herd B, respectively. Assessment of postpartum health
Herds were visited and cows examined twice weekly 
by the research team, and the data collection scheme is presented in Figure 1. Cows were enrolled 21 ± 3 d be-fore their expected parturition (257 ± 3 d of gestation). At enrollment, they were assessed for body condition score (BCS; 5-point scale with 0.25-point increments; Ferguson et al., 1994) and lameness (score ≥3 on a 1 to 5 scale; Sprecher et al., 1997). Cows were tested and had a negative result for bovine viral diarrhea at time of enrolment using a commercial ELISA kit (BVDV PI X2, IDEXX Laboratories Inc., Westbrook, ME). After calving, blood samples were taken through the middle coccygeal vein or artery at 2 ± 2 (1 to 4) and 6 ± 2 (4 to 7) DIM to measure serum concentrations of total Ca, Hp, and NEFA as described below. At 4, 8, 11, and 15 ± 2 DIM, β-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) concentra-tions were measured in blood using a hand-held device (Precision Xtra Meter and B-ketone test strips, Abbott Laboratories) and vaginal discharge was assessed us-ing an intravaginal device (Metricheck, Simcro). Cows with a fetid, watery, and brownish or reddish discharge were classified as having metritis regardless of rectal temperature. Cows were checked for retained placenta (RP) during sampling, examinations, and routinely by the farm personnel.
At 35 ± 3 DIM, cows were examined for mucopu-
rulent or purulent vaginal discharge [PVD; score 2 or 3 as in Sheldon et al. (2006)] and for endometritis (ENDO) using an intrauterine cytobrush technique. The endometrial cytology samples were collected from the dorsal aspect of the uterine body and smeared on glass microscope slides, stained with a modified Wright-Giemsa protocol (Hema 3 Stat Pack; Fisher Scientific), and examined under an optical microscope at 400 × magnification. The proportion of polymorphonuclear cells (% PMN) of 300 total nucleated cells counted per slide was recorded. Serum P4 concentrations were measured in samples taken at 21, 35, 49, and 63 ± 3 DIM to estimate the onset of first ovulation when the first sample was ≥1.0 ng/mL. Cows were examined for lameness at 21 and 49 ± 3 DIM, and for BCS at 63 ± 3 DIM. All scores were assigned by the first or sec-ond author and the interobserver agreements (Cohen’s weighted Kappa) were over 80%. Additional health data recorded by the farm staff were obtained from Dairy-Comp including calving ease, RP, milk fever, displaced abomasum, and mastitis (with a recorded treatment). Cows were treated according to each farm’s predefined protocols. Clinical mastitis cases were treated with in-tramammary antibiotics, and cows with RP (herd A), RP with fever (herd B), or metritis were treated with injectable antibiotics. Cows with BHB ≥1.2 mM were administered oral propylene glycol daily for 3 to 5 d and injectable polyvitamins. Multiparous cows received 2 oral Ca boluses after calving, and cases of milk fever (difficulty standing or walking associated with hypo-calcemia by 3 DIM) were treated with intravenous and subcutaneous Ca. Hoof trimming occurred at dry-off and at mid-lactation, and visibly lame cows identified by the staff were hoof trimmed or treated with inject-able antibiotics (for foot rot).
Reproductive management for first insemination
First postpartum AI occurred primarily based on es-
trus detection by automated activity monitors. Herd A used a pedometer system (AfiAct, Afimilk, Kibutz Afi-kim) and, starting at 55 DIM, farm personnel checked for estrus alerts twice daily and inseminated cows with-in 12 h of the alert (AM or PM). Cows not detected in estrus by 73 ± 3 DIM were administered PGF
2α 
(Bioestrovet, Vetoquinol or Lutalyse, Zoetis), and if not detected in estrus by 87 ± 3 DIM, entered a timed AI program [Ovsynch: GnRH (Fertiline, Vetoquinol) – 7 d – PGF
2α – 56 h – GnRH – 12 h – AI]. Cows initiating 
the protocol without a CL received an intravaginal P4 releasing device (CIDR; Zoetis) from protocol d 0 to d 7. Herd B used a collar (SmartTag Neck, Nedap) for estrus detection and cows received AI in the AM within 24 h after an estrus alert, starting at 50 DIM. Primipa-rous cows not detected in estrus by 78 ± 3 DIM entered an Ovsynch program if a CL was present, or received GnRH (Fertiline, Vetoquinol) followed by Ovsynch 7 d later. Multiparous cows not detected in estrus by 60 ± 3 DIM were administered PGF
2α and, if not detected in 
estrus 7 d later, entered either an Ovsynch (if a CL was present) or received GnRH followed by Ovsynch 7 d later (if no CL). In both herds, pregnancy diagnosis by transrectal ultrasound occurred once at approximately 33 ± 3 d after AI.
