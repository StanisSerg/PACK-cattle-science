ABSTRACT
Recent work has established that proper classification 
of cows based on their periparturient status has impor -
tant implications for risks of health, production, and 
reproduction outcomes during the postpartum period. However, the dynamics of blood calcium (Ca) status and inflammation are far less understood. The objective of this study was to determine the relationship between subclinical hypocalcemia (SCH) dynamics and peripar -
turient circulating serum amyloid-A (SAA), haptoglo-bin (Hp), tumor necrosis factor-α (TNFα), interferon-γ (IFNγ), and interleukin-10 (IL-10) concentrations, DMI, and milk production during the first 9 wk of lactation. Data collected from multiparous Holstein cows (n = 96) were retrospectively classified into 1 of 4 calcemia groups based on blood concentrations of total Ca (tCa) at 1 and 5 DIM: normocalcemic (NC; [tCa] >1.95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and >2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 53); transient SCH (tSCH; [tCa] ≤1. 95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and >2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 15); delayed SCH (dSCH; [tCa] >1. 95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and ≤2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 15); and persistent SCH (pSCH; [tCa] ≤1. 95 mmol at 1 DIM and ≤2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 13). The MIXED procedure of SAS (v. 9.4, SAS Institute Inc.) was used to analyze pre- (−28 and −1 d relative to calving) and postpartum (1, 3, 5, and 7 DIM) blood mineral concentra-tions, SAA, Hp, cytokines, milk production, and differ -
ences between Ca groups, using repeated measures for time. Prepartum blood concentrations of IL-10 tended to be greater in tSCH cows compared with NC, dSCH, and pSCH. Postpartum concentrations of Hp, SAA, and TNFα were different by Ca group. Concentrations of SAA and Hp were highest in the pSCH group at d 3, and TNFα was highest for cows categorized as dSCH, irrespective of time. Overall, our results suggest that cows experienc-ing different SCH dynamics display different patterns of inflammatory markers and that elevated concentrations of proinflammatory biomarkers accompany some catego-ries of hypocalcemia during the early lactation period.Key words: subclinical, dyscalcemia, transition, inflammation
INTRODUCTION
The transition from late gestation to the onset of early 
lactation is a critical point of adaptation to rapid meta-bolic changes. This period of transition for the dairy cow presents a set of challenges, and her success is dependent upon her ability to adapt efficiently. This time is typically marked by a significant decline in DMI prepartum and postpartum DMI, failing to meet the energetic and nu-trient demands of milk production (Bauman and Currie, 1980). Simultaneously, rapid increases in Ca demands are often cited as particularly stringent with a 2- to 3-fold surge in demand at parturition (Drackley et al., 2005; Goff et al., 2014). Clinical hypocalcemia is relatively well-controlled in confinement dairy systems (Reinhardt et al., 2011). The subclinical iteration of hypocalcemia, subclinical hypocalcemia (SCH), is more prevalent, and its presence past 1 DIM is considered to put cows at in-creased risk for other periparturient disorders, decreased postpartum DMI, poor reproductive performance, and milk production losses (Neves et al., 2018; McArt and Neves, 2020; Seely and McArt, 2022). With this in mind, recent work has established 4 distinct categories of SCH in cows based upon their lactation, health and culling outcomes, and postpartum Ca dynamics on 1, 2, and 4 DIM (dependent on parity; McArt and Neves, 2020). These groups, normocalcemia (NC), transient SCH (tSCH), delayed SCH (dSCH), and persistent SCH (pSCH), are notably different in their relative risks for 
disease, production, and reproductive outcomes (Neves et al., 2018; McArt and Neves, 2020).Associations between periparturient calcium dynamics of multiparous Holstein cows and inflammation markers during the transition period

Journal of Dairy Science Vol. TBC No. TBC, TBCAs gestation comes to an end and lactogenesis begins, 
acute inflammation is considered normal if it is limited 
in duration and severity (Bradford and Swartz, 2020). Released from macrophages and tissues, inflammatory mediators are intimately involved in the rapid metabolic changes occurring during transition, including increased lipid mobilization, alterations in endocrine function, and increased oxidative stress (Sordillo and Aitken, 2009; Trevisi et al., 2015). However, an uncontrolled or unre-solving inflammatory response can be detrimental to a successful transition for the cow, resulting in increased risk for periparturient disease, increased culling, and decreased production (Drackley, 1999; Bertoni et al., 2015; Bradford et al., 2015). Whereas SCH is understood to increase risk of other disease events, it has also been observed that rapid declines in blood Ca concentra-tion are a conserved response to an activated immune response (Collage et al., 2013). Increased intracellular Ca concentration is a key feature of immune response, and several researchers have found associations between severe inflammatory responses and decreased blood Ca concentrations postpartum (Kimura et al., 2006; Bertoni et al., 2008).
Given the serious outcomes of cows experiencing un-
regulated inflammation and Ca dysregulation, as well as the existing relationship between the two, we must seek more consistent definitions for diagnosis of SCH and de-velop more robust data sets to describe acute phase pro-teins and cytokines in the bovine. Recently, more work has been conducted (Neves, 2023; Seminara et al., 2025) to better understand the impetus of periparturient hypo-calcemia and whether it is solely driven by immune sys-tem activation (Horst et al., 2021). We need to elucidate the nature of the relationships between periparturient in-flammatory status and Ca dynamics. It would be prudent to investigate how these different Ca dynamics relate to the dynamics of periparturient inflammation. Therefore, the objective of this study was to assess the patterns of select acute phase proteins, specifically haptoglobin (Hp) and serum amyloid-A (SAA), and cytokines pre- 
and postpartum in cows retrospectively assigned to 1 of 4 calcemia groups based on total calcium (tCa) status at 1 and 5 DIM. We hypothesized that SAA, Hp, TNFα, IFNγ, and IL-10 would be tightly associated with calcium group assignment, with greatest elevations of SAA, Hp, and proinflammatory cytokines in SCH cows postpartum and greatest elevations of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 in normal and tSCH cows.MATERIALS AND METHODS
Study Population and Experimental Design
All procedures involving animals were approved by 
the Cornell University Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee, protocol no. 2019-0036. Our analysis was completed as a retrospective cohort design and in-cluded multiparous Holstein cows (n = 96) during the periparturient period from a previous study (Graef et al., 2025). All animals were housed between October 2019 and October 2020 in individual tiestalls at the Cornell University Ruminant Center (Harford, NY).
Feeding Management and Health Monitoring
All cows were individually fed once per day between 
0700 and 0900 h for lactating cows and between 0700 and 1100 h for dry cows. Cows were all fed the same far-off dry cow and fresh cow diets after parturition. All far-off dry cow diet, close-up diet, and postpartum diet compositions were reported in Graef et al. (2025).
Cows were observed daily throughout the trial for 
health disorders by farm and research personnel. All postpartum cows underwent daily physical exams by the research team until 10 DIM; these included temperature, respiration rate, rumination, and metritis checks. Identi-fication of health disorders by farm staff was as follows: clinical hypocalcemia = decline in blood Ca accompa-nied by clinical signs of cold extremities, trembling, and paresis; metritis = watery, fetid brown uterine discharge with or without systemic signs; retained fetal membranes = retention of fetal membranes for >24 h; mastitis = abnormal milk or an inflamed quarter; displaced aboma-sum = a “ping” sound upon simultaneous percussion and auscultation of the abdomen; elevated urine ketones (Ketostix, Bayer Corporation), cows with urine ketones measuring “small” or greater for 2 consecutive days were treated with 300 mL of oral propylene glycol for 5 d. No cows included in this study received supplemental Ca in the form of oral boluses, pastes, gels, or intravenous infusion; nor did they receive any antibiotics or anti-in-flammatories. Body weight was measured and BCS was assigned weekly (1 to 5 scale with 0.25-point intervals; Edmonson et al., 1989) by 2 scorers and averaged for statistical analysis.
Cows were milked 3×/d at 0600, 1400, and 2200 h 
from calving through 9 wk postpartum. Milk weights were collected from each milking, and milk samples were collected once per week from all 3 milkings within a 24-h period. Blood samples were collected from the coccygeal 
vein or artery, and methods are described in Graef et al. 
(2025). Prepartum samples were taken at d −28 (−28.5 ± SD 2.6) and d −1 (−1.5 ± SD 0.5) relative to expected calving, and postpartum samples at d 1, 2, 3, 5, and 7 rel-ative to calving. Prepartum blood samples were collected between 0600 and 0730 h. Postpartum blood samples were collected at ~0700 h. All handling of blood samples and analysis for tCa, Mg, and P analysis is described in Graef et al. (2025). Pre- and postpartum serum was analyzed for Hp using a protocol described by Eckersall et al. (1999) with inter- and intra-assay CV of 8.0% and 3.0%, respectively, and SAA with a commercially avail-able colorimetric kit (Tri-Delta Diagnostics Inc., Town-ship, NJ), reporting intra- and interassay CV of 9.6% and 13.3%, respectively. Assays were run in duplicate (Hp) and triplicate (SAA). Spectrophotometric measurements were conducted using a tunable microplate reader (Spec-traMax 190, Molecular Devices, San Jose, CA). Serum TNFα, IFNγ, and IL-10 were analyzed via a bead-based multiplex assay developed and described by Sipka et al. (2022). Intra- and interassay CV , respectively, are as fol-lows: 4.9% and 9.0% for TNFα, 7.6% and 7.0% for IFNγ, and 6.0% and 3.0% for IL-10.
Statistical Analysis
All statistical analyses were conducted using SAS soft-
ware (v. 9.4, SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC). Descriptive statistics were generated using the FREQ and MEANS procedures. Before statistical analysis of DMI, milk yield, and blood analytes, cows were retrospectively classified into 1 of 4 calcemia groups. Other work has done this based on tCa status at 1 and 4 DIM (McArt and Neves, 2020; Seely et al., 2021; McCray et al., 2023). This retrospective cohort study did not have a blood sample from 4 DIM and, seeking to continue to prioritize the established directionality of these calcemia groupings, instead based classification on blood tCa con-centrations at 1 and 5 DIM. To diagnose SCH, this study used a previously identified cutpoint of 1.95 mmol/L at 1 DIM (Seely et al., 2021), whereas for 5 DIM we identified the interquartile range of tCa concentrations and divided that range into 23 cutpoints in 0.02 mmol/L intervals. Generalized linear mixed models were created via the MIXED procedure for parity 2 and ≥3, respec-tively, to establish milk production differences between cows above and below the tCa cutpoint being assessed (Table 1). Models included fixed effects of week of lacta-tion, 5 DIM Ca status, and their interaction. To identify the maximum differences in milk production, final tCa cutpoints were selected. Cows below the cutpoint were deemed SCH and produced less milk than cows above the cutpoint. This definition of diagnosis was selected based on our understanding that cows experiencing delayed or persistent SCH produce less milk than cows considered to have a normal tCa concentration at 4 DIM (Neves et al., 2018; McArt and Neves, 2020; Seely et al., 2021). The calcemia groups were as follows: NC (tCa >1.95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and >2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 53); tSCH (tCa ≤1.95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and >2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 15); dSCH (tCa >1.95 mmol/L at 1 DIM and ≤2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 15); and pSCH (tCa ≤1.95 mmol at 1 DIM and ≤2.32 mmol/L at 5 DIM, n = 13). A post hoc power calculation was conducted using ClinCalc (ClinCalc LLC, https:
 / / clincalc  .com). Allowing 
for a 5% risk of type I error, our sample size reported 92.4% power to detect a difference of 3 kg/d (±1.2 kg/d) in daily milk yield.
Generalized linear mixed models were created through 
the MIXED procedure. Pre- and postpartum samples were assessed separately and included their respective covari-ates. Models included the REPEATED statement to ac-count for multiple measures to assess changes over time and detect differences between calcemia groups for blood measures, milk yield, and DMI. Seven covariance struc-tures were tested for each model, including compound symmetry, heterogeneous compound symmetry, autore-gressive order 1, heterogeneous autoregressive order 1, antedependence 1, variance components, and Toeplitz where the lowest Akaike’s information criterion was se-lected: variance components (Littell et al., 1996). When non-normality of residual variance was evident (Hp), data were log-transformed and analysis repeated. Results are reported as least squares means or back-transformed means for transformed variables, and their respective 95% confidence intervals.
In all results, differences between means were consid-
ered statistically significant when P  ≤ 0.05 with evidence 
for a difference when 0.05 < P ≤ 0.10. When main ef-
fects and least squares means differed between groups, Tukey-Kramer studentized adjustments were used to ac-count for multiple comparisons. All models included the random effect of cow nested within enrollment block and the fixed effects of calcemia group, previously assigned treatment, time, parity, and their respective 2- and 3-way interactions. Parity was defined as either second or third and greater. Variables and interactions were removed in a backward stepwise manner if P  > 0.10. Irrespective of 
statistical significance, main effects of time, calcemia group, and their interaction remained in all models. Prepartum treatment assignment was not significant in any model, pre- or postpartum. Parity only remained in the model for postpartum P, milk yield (MY), ECM, and FCM. DMI and Milk Yield
Pre- and postpartum DMI are reported in Table 3 and 
Figure 1. Prepartum DMI was greater in tSCH cows 
(13.2 kg/d, 95% CI = 12.2, 14.2 kg/d) compared with NC (12.8 kg/d, 95% CI = 12.3, 13.2 kg/d) and pSCH (11.8 kg/d, 95% CI = 11.2, 12.4 kg/d) cows. Prepartum DMI decreased by day as cows approached parturition (P  < 
0.001). In the first 3 wk postpartum, DMI of cows in the pSCH group were considered lowest by SCH and time, particularly at 2, 6, 7, 15, and 19 DIM (Figure 1; P < 
0.001). Over 9 wk, postpartum DMI steadily increased (P < 0.001) but otherwise did not differ by calcemia group (P = 0.54) or parity (P  = 0.84), and the interaction of 
calcemia group and week was not statistically significant (P = 0.33).
Results for MY and ECM are reported in Table 3 and 
Figure 2. From wk 1 to 3, numerical differences in MY (P = 0.06) and significant differences in ECM (P  = 0.02) 
indicated the greatest yields in tSCH and dSCH groups, respectively, compared with the other 3 groups. From wk 1 to 9, cows categorized as tSCH had the highest MY and ECM, whereas pSCH cows had the lowest MY and ECM (P < 0.001). Cows categorized as dSCH outperformed 
NC and pSCH cows in MY and pSCH cows in both MY and ECM in wk 1 to 9 (P  < 0.001).
