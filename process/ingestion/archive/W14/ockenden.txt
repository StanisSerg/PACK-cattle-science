ABSTRACT

INTRODUCTION

We aimed to assess the effects of preweaning nutrition
and postweaning growth rates on the metabolic characteristics of replacement dairy heifers until 20 mo of
age. At birth, heifers were allocated to 1 of 4 treatment
groups, which were a combination of preweaning (high
or low preweaning nutritional treatment) and postweaning treatments (high or low postweaning growth rate).
In the preweaning phase (birth until weaning at 10 wk
of age), heifers were subject to either a high (8 L/d) or
a low (4 L/d) milk feeding strategy; then, at weaning,
either a high or a low postweaning growth rate. Heifers in the high preweaning treatment groups recorded
greater growth rates until weaning compared with heifers
in the low preweaning treatment groups. Heifers in the
high preweaning groups consumed approximately twice
the amount of milk and half the amount of concentrate.
Blood samples taken at 6 wk of age had lower BHB and
higher glucose and IGF-1 concentrations in the high
preweaning treatment in the preweaning phase; these differences disappeared postweaning. Additionally, insulin
levels were also higher, and quantitative insulin sensitivity check index (QUICKI) results were lower for heifers
on the high preweaning treatment and for those on the
high postweaning treatment at 6 wk and 13 mo of age.
No differences between treatments were detected at 8 mo
of age. Results indicate a positive effect of accelerated
preweaning nutrition and postweaning growth rate (at
13 mo of age) on these metabolic biomarkers; however,
these treatment effects were independent of one another
and reflected the nutritional input at the time.
Key words: preweaning nutrition, postweaning nutrition,
accelerated milk feeding, heifer calf development

Optimizing the growth rate of heifers (female cows
yet to calve) provides opportunity to improve lifetime
production and resilience of dairy cows. A cow that is
more resilient will suffer fewer metabolic or pathogenicassociated diseases, consistently produce high levels of
milk, and live longer in the herd. Improving lifetime
production per cow can increase the economic profitability of enterprises while reducing the environmental
impact of the industry (De Vries, 2017; Grandl et al.,
2019). Optimal heifer growth is defined through target
weights that set the ideal weight of a heifer at various
stages of her development to maximize her productivity
and longevity. The Australian industry target weight for
dairy heifers is 85% of mature liveweight at first calving
(Dairy Australia, 2020), with the presumption that bigger
heifers at calving are more productive over their lifetime
(Macdonald et al., 2005; Dairy Australia, 2020).
The suitability of rearing strategies and intermittent
growth targets for replacement dairy heifers in Australia are unclear. Although an ideal target weight of
85% mature liveweight at calving is known to increase
productivity and longevity within the herd, how to best
achieve this end point is not specified. Recent research
has focused on accelerated milk feeding of heifers in the
preweaning phase, but little effort has been made beyond
weaning, particularly in combination with the postweaning growth phase. Further, there is limited understanding
of the ongoing physiological effects or consequences of
the accelerated growth phase on future growth and resilience. Compensatory growth is a phenomenon often
observed in ruminants after periods of feed restriction
(Owens et al., 1993). Yet, it is not understood whether
this compensation influences the development of physiological mechanisms including immunity and metabolism,
as well as BW.
In the preweaning phase, the calf transitions into a
functioning ruminant. Before this transition, energy is
supplied primarily in the form of glucose and fatty acids

begins to dominate, the energy supply changes to VFA
(Quigley et al., 1991; Baldwin et al., 2004; Khan et al.,
2011b). During the preweaning phase, high concentrations of BHB have been used in calf nutritional studies
as a proxy indicator for concentrate intake and rumen
development (Khan et al., 2011a; Byrne et al., 2017).
As adults, in addition to BHB, nonesterified fatty acid
(NEFA) is also used as an indicator of mobilization of
body reserves and energy balance in response to the
high energy demands of lactation (Taylor et al., 2003;
Chandler et al., 2018; Tessari et al., 2020). Higher IGF-1
concentrations have been reported with increased ADG
and ME intake and positively associated with advanced
growth rates and organ development in calves (Vicari
et al., 2008; Rosadiuk et al., 2021; Wilms et al., 2022).
During lactation, the concentration of IGF-1 is said to
decrease during prolonged periods of energy deficit
(McGuire et al., 1995), and low concentrations have also
been associated with detrimental effects on fertility (Taylor et al., 2003). Studies have also investigated nutrient
partitioning leading to the selection of cows with optimal
genetic partitioning characteristics for milk production
(Oltenacu and Broom, 2010). However, environmental
factors, such as preweaning nutrition, also result in permanent changes to metabolic function (Opsomer et al.,
2016), yet the mechanisms and long-term preservation of
these changes have not been fully investigated.
This experiment examined the effects of 4 early-life
growth paths on growth and metabolic characteristics
of Holstein-Friesian replacement dairy heifers as a surrogate for future resilience. The metabolic biomarkers
selected are metabolites frequently used throughout the
literature to examine the current metabolic state of dairy
cows and calves. Postweaning treatment design was
based upon the current Australian heifer rearing strategy,
with fixed BW targets set to achieve a predetermined
precalving BW. It is one of the few longer-term development studies in dairy cows with a focus on a combinaJournal of Dairy Science Vol. 108 No. 4, 2025

tion of varying pre- and postweaning rearing strategies.
This will enable us not only to establish any potential
benefits on resilience from feeding more milk to calves
in the preweaning phase but also to evaluate the benefits
of varying growth rates in the postweaning phase, in particular whether faster postweaning growth can overcome
any potential negative effects on resilience from feeding less milk in the preweaning phase. The hypotheses
tested were (1) that in the preweaning phase, heifers that
receive a high milk allowance will have greater growth
rates than heifers that receive a low milk allowance until
weaning; and (2) that heifers that receive a high milk allowance preweaning will have physiologically superior
metabolic characteristics than heifers that receive a low
milk allowance preweaning until 13 mo of age regardless
of postweaning growth rate.
MATERIALS AND METHODS

The preweaning phase of this experiment was undertaken at the Agriculture Victoria Dairy Research Centre,
Ellinbank, Victoria, Australia (38°14′S,145°56′E), in
collaboration with the University of Melbourne, from
July to October 2021 (Australian spring). Postweaning, the heifers were transported to Agriculture Victoria Dairy Research Centre’s heifer rearing contractor
property, located 20 km away, for the remainder of the
experiment. Approval to proceed was granted by the
Agricultural Research and Extension Animal Ethics
Committee, application number 2021-08. Experimental
procedures were conducted in accordance with the Australian Code of Practice for the Care and Use of Animals
for Scientific Purposes (National Health and Medical
Research Council, 2013).
Experimental Design and Treatments

A total of 80 spring-born Holstein-Friesian replacement heifers were enrolled at birth and remained under

Ockenden et al.: PRE- AND POSTWEANING NUTRITIONAL STRATEGIES

experimental conditions until approximately 20 mo of
age. Heifers were randomly assigned to 1 of 4 treatments
at birth (treatment titles signify preweaning treatment
and postweaning growth rate, respectively):
1. High–high (HH)
2. High–low (HL)
3. Low–high (LH)
4. Low–low (LL)
Treatments were balanced for heifers’ birth weight, parity
of the dam, and estimated Balanced Performance Index
(BPI; Australia’s national selection index). All BPI were
calculated before birth using the average of the parents’
BPI. As treatments neared full allocation, these parameters were checked to ensure they remained balanced.
In any instance where they were not balanced between
treatments, randomization was overruled. Calves were
separated into 4 groups by date of birth (i.e., the first
20 calves were allocated to group 1, the following 20
calves born were allocated to group 2, and so on). Each
group was housed in a separate pen and followed their
own timeline, with 5 calves of each treatment per group
or pen; thus, pen being a blocking factor, and design of
the experiment being randomized complete block design.
During the preweaning phase, the assigned 80 heifers
underwent 1 of 2 nutritional treatments:
1. High preweaning treatment (HH and HL): 8-L milk
allocation daily (40 heifers).
2. Low preweaning treatment (LH and LL): 4-L milk
allocation daily (40 heifers).
At weaning, the predetermined postweaning strategies
were then evenly distributed within each of the preweaning treatments, giving rise to the 4 experimental treatments of 20 heifers. Heifers followed either a high or
a low postweaning growth rate. Postweaning treatment
design was based on current Australian heifer rearing
strategies and were defined by set target weights. Target
weights were applied according to the group average.
Heifers followed 4 growth trajectories toward 1 of 2
final precalving weights. Target weights were based on
the historical data form the Ellinbank Research Farm
dairy herd and the ideal weight range of 85% mature
liveweight at first calving. The precalving target weights
were as follows:
1. High postweaning growth rate (HH and LH): 600 kg.
2. Low postweaning growth rate (HL and LL): 530 kg.
To achieve this in the first 9 mo after weaning, the LH
group was grown at a faster rate to catch up to the HH
group, and the HL group’s growth was restricted to meet

the LL group’s lower average BW. Heifers completed the
experiment at 20 mo of age.
Animal Management

At birth, each heifer was fitted with an individual identification ear tag as part of normal farm practice. These
tags also allowed for identification of the individual calf
within the automatic feeding system. Heifers were group
housed within four pens (20 heifers per pen). Each of the 4
pens contained an equal number (n = 5) of heifers assigned
to each of the 4 treatments. Pens were filled sequentially
by date of birth to allow for age-appropriate grouping.
Heifers were separated from their dams and given 4 L
of pooled fresh colostrum with >22% Brix index within
8 h of birth. Before enrolment, at 24 to 48 h after birth,
successful passive transfer of immunity was confirmed
using blood serum Brix refractometer methods (de Souza
et al., 2021). Following the initial colostrum feeding, the
first 4 milk meals fed to all heifers was 2 L of colostrum
milk (milk from the second to the fourth milking after
birth) via individual teat feeders within nursery pens (up
to 5 heifers per pen). Heifers were then transferred to
the experimental calf rearing facility, where they were
fed whole saleable milk via automatic calf feeders (Lely
Calm; Lely Australia, Truganina, Victoria, Australia) according to their treatment allocation. Heifers in the high
treatment group were gradually accustomed to the higher
volume of milk, by capping the milk on the first day to
6 L, then 8 L daily after that. Milk meal size was capped
at 2 L per feed with a defined 2-h gap between meals
for both treatment groups to avoid overconsumption. The
automatic feeder software recorded the individual daily
intake of milk for each calf.
All heifers were allowed up to 3 kg of a commercial
calf concentrate (Reid Stockfeeds–Gippsland, Trafalgar,
Victoria, Australia) per day via calf automatic concentrate feeders (Lely Calm; Lely Australia, Truganina,
Victoria, Australia). Individual daily concentrate intake
was recorded by the feeder software. Water and a lucerne
hay (Medicago sativa) were available ad libitum within
each pen. The intake of water and hay was not recorded
for this experiment.
Heifers were weighed using electronic scales at 24 to
48 h of age, representing birth BW. Body weight measurements were then taken on a weekly basis until weaning.
Heifers were weaned over 10 d from 10 wk of age. The
automatic feeders were programmed to reduce the milk
allocation by 10% daily over 10 d with 1 L remaining
on the final day. Once weaning was completed, heifers
entered the postweaning phase of the experiment.
In the postweaning phase, heifers were run as a single
herd. All heifers had access to grazed pasture as the
majority of the diet. In instances where pasture was

Ockenden et al.: PRE- AND POSTWEANING NUTRITIONAL STRATEGIES

limited (due to seasonal availability), pasture silage
was fed out in the paddock. Additionally, heifers also
had access to a commercial concentrate (Reids Stockfeeds, Calf Weaner until 12 mo of age, then Yearling
Mix from 12 mo onward) via an in-paddock automatic
concentrate feeder (Velos Cow Feeding Station, Nedap
N.V. Livestock Management, Groenlo, the Netherlands).
Individual daily intake was recorded by the feeder software. Individual concentrate rations were adjusted after
each weighing to ensure treatment growth trajectories
stayed on target. Pasture was restricted for all heifers in
instances where any one treatment group was exceeding
treatment target weights, using strip grazing methods.
Heifers were weighed on electronic scales fortnightly
for the first 6 mo at pasture and then monthly for the
remainder of the experiment.
Nutritional Measurements

During the preweaning phase, a representative milk
sample of approximately 20 mL was collected fortnightly and sent to a commercial laboratory (HICO Laboratories, Korumburra, Victoria, Australia). Samples were
analyzed to determine fat, protein, and lactose concentrations. The mean nutritive concentration of whole milk
fed to heifers throughout the preweaning period was 3.4
± 0.44% (mean ± SD) fat, 3.3 ± 0.21% protein, and 4.8
± 0.12% lactose.
During the preweaning phase, representative samples
of concentrate and hay were taken each fortnight. In the
postweaning phase, representative samples of concentrate, silage, and pasture were collected monthly. Pasture
samples were initially washed and then left to dry at
4°C for 2 h before processing. For processing, half of
all samples were dried at 105°C for 48 h to determine
the percentage DM. The remaining half was oven-dried
at 60°C for 48 h before being ground through a 1-mm
sieve and sent to a commercial laboratory for wet chemistry analysis (Dairy One Forage Laboratory, Ithaca,
NY; Dairy One, 2023). The nutritive characteristics are
presented in Table 1.
Blood Collection and Biomarker Analyses

At 6 wk, 8 mo, and 13 mo of age, blood samples were
taken from each heifer to determine the concentration of
metabolic biomarkers. In the preweaning phase, access
to milk and concentrate feeders were restricted for 2 h
before blood sampling. No fasting was implemented for
either postweaning blood samples. Blood samples were
collected in evacuated tubes containing lithium heparin,
EDTA, or fluoride/oxalate, as well as plain evacuated
tubes. Lithium heparin, EDTA, and fluoride/oxalate tubes
were stored on ice before centrifugation at 1,578 × g for

15 min at 4°C. Plain evacuated tubes were incubated at
24°C for 2 h before centrifugation at 1,258 × g for 15 min
at 24°C. Plasma and serum samples were then stored at
−20°C before analyses.
Metabolic biomarker analyses included BHB, NEFA
glucose, insulin, and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF1). The BHB, NEFA, and glucose assays were performed
using Catachem Inc. reagents, controls and calibrators
as per manufacturer’s instructions, on a ChemWell 2910

Victoria, Australia). Insulin and IGF-1 assays were developed and conducted at the Assay Centre, School of
Agriculture, Food and Ecosystems Sciences, the University of Melbourne, Parkville, Australia. Insulin concentrations were measured in a homologous double antibody
radioimmunoassay (RIA). The RIA was performed using
purified insulin antiserum raised in guinea pig (Antibodies Australian) and purified bovine insulin for iodination
and standard (Sigma-Aldrich, cat. no. I5500). Full assay
methods can be found in Ockenden et al. (2023).
Bovine plasma IGF-1 concentration was measured
following sample extraction in a homologous double
antibody RIA. The RIA was performed using human
IGF-1 antiserum raised in rabbit (National Hormone
and Peptide Program [NHPP] AFP4892898), and human IGF-1 for iodination and standard (GroPep, Human
IGF-1, receptor grade). To extract the sample, 200 µL
of acid/ethanol extraction solution (0.25 M HCl in 100%
ethanol) was added to 50 µL of sample, vortexed, and
then incubated at room temperature for 30 min. Following incubation, the sample was centrifuged at 10,000 × g
for 20 min at 4°C and the supernatant collected, to which
100 µL of 0.885 M Tris was added. After mixing, the
sample was stored at −20°C for 60 min and then centrifuged again at 10,000 × g for 20 min at 4°C with this
final supernatant collected for IGF-1 assay. For the assay,
200 µL of assay buffer (0.5% BSA/0.03 M sodium phosphate monobasic/0.2 M sodium phosphate dibasic/0.1%
sodium azide/0.1% triton X) and 200 µL of first antibody
(1:20,000) diluted in 1:300 normal rabbit serum was
added to duplicate plastic tubes containing either 100 µL
of standard (0.1638–40 ng/mL) or 100 µL of extracted
sample. After preincubation at 4°C for 72 h, 125I iodinated human IGF-1 was added (15,000 cpm/100 µL), and
the incubation continued for a further 24 h at 4°C. The
antibody-bound hormone was separated from the free
hormone by the addition of 200 µL of goat-anti-rabbit
(1:75). The tubes were incubated with the second antibody overnight at 4°C before centrifugation (10,000 × g,
30 min, 4°C), after which the supernatant was aspirated
and the precipitate counted in a gamma counter. Insulin
sensitivity was determined using the quantitative insulin
sensitivity check index (QUICKI) with the following
equation (Singh and Saxena, 2010):

Statistical Analysis

The effects of treatment on growth rates, nutritive intakes, and metabolic biomarkers were examined using
linear mixed models (LMM) in GenStat 22nd Edition
(VSN International, Hemel Hempstead, UK). The LMM
were fitted using restricted maximum likelihood with
individual calf as the unit of analysis. The fixed effects
included the main effect of the preweaning diet, main effect of the postweaning diet, main effect of time factor,
and their 2- and 3-way interactions. The effects of covariates (birth BW, estimated BPI, and age of the dam)
were also fitted as fixed effects. The effects of blocking
factor pen and calf within pen were fitted as random effects. The random term calf within pen was used as residual term. The random effects were assumed to be
normally distributed with zero mean and constant variance. The LMM used to analyze data in the preweaning
phase differed from the overall model in that it included
treatment groups, time, and their interaction as fixed effects. However, the covariates and random effects structure remained the same as in the overall model. Normality of residuals was checked using histograms and plots
of residuals versus fitted values. The significant differences among treatment groups at a time point are presented using Duncan’s letters based on the LSD test,

Growth Rates

At birth, no significant differences in BW appeared
between the treatment groups (Figure 1). From 1 wk of
age the high milk-fed treatment groups (HH and HL)
were significantly heavier than the low milk-fed treatment groups (LH and LL; wk 1: P = 0.017; wk 2–wk 11:
P < 0.001). The separation between the 2 growth paths
increased until weaning at 12 wk of age, resulting in approximately a 15-kg difference in BW (P < 0.001).
Deliberately, in the postweaning phase (Figure 2) the
HL and LH treatment groups’ BW converged at between
8 and 9 mo of age and began to separate again after 11 mo
of age. At 13 mo, postweaning treatment groups aligned
(HH with LH and HL with LL), and the difference in
BW was consistent until 15 mo of age, per experimental
design. However, after 15 mo of age, the heifers from the
high preweaning treatments had greater ADG than those
from the low preweaning treatments, and BW started to
separate again.
Feed Intake

Daily milk intake for the high treatment groups (HH
and HL) was higher than the low treatment groups
(LH and LL) throughout the preweaning period (Table
2). During the first week, heifers in the low milk-fed
groups consumed slightly under their allocation (LH:
3.95 ± 0.020 L/calf per day; LL: 3.83 ± 0.040 L/calf
per day), but in all subsequent weeks they consumed
their 4-L allocation daily until weaning started at 10 wk
of age. Heifers in the high treatment groups consumed
almost twice this, with 6.94 ± 0.118 L daily in the
first week, followed by ≥7.6 L/calf per day for the HL
group; and 7.22 ± 0.117 in wk 1, followed by ≥7.6 L/
calf per day for the HH group for all other weeks until
weaning commenced at 10 wk of age. After weaning
commenced, milk intakes reduced by 10% per day for
10 d, with 1 L remaining on d 10 and no milk allowance on d 11 of weaning. Throughout the preweaning
period, the total average amount of milk consumed by
heifers in the low milk-fed group was 298 L, containing
10.1 kg of CP, 834 MJ of ME, and 10.43 kg of crude
fat (CF). The high milk-fed heifers consumed a total
average of 582 L, containing 19.4 kg of CP, 1,595 MJ
of ME, and 20.4 kg of CF.

were significantly different between the low and high
milk-fed groups from 4 wk of age (Table 3). Both low

DISCUSSION

In the preweaning phase, heifers from the 4 treatment
groups followed their respective milk diets’ growth paths.
Calves fed a higher volume of milk had greater growth
rates than those on the restricted milk diet, supporting
our first hypothesis. It is well understood that restricted
milk-fed calves will consume greater quantities of concentrate to try to compensate for the nutrients lacking
from the limited milk supply (Jasper and Weary, 2002;
Sweeney et al., 2010). Calf concentrate intakes in this
experiment reflected this, with the low milk-fed calves
consuming significantly greater amounts of concentrate
in the preweaning phase (P < 0.001; Table 3). Thus, their
combined total CP intake (from milk, as seen in Table 2,
and preweaning concentrates as seen in Table 3) for the
preweaning phase was close to that of the high milk-fed
calves, consuming a total of only approximately 12%
less CP in this period. However, they were not able to
compensate for ME or CF intake, consuming almost 25%
less ME and 50% less CF than the high milk-fed calves
in the preweaning period. This further indicates that, although the restricted fed calves attempted to compensate
nutritionally for the limited supply of milk with increased
concentrate intake, this was not fully achieved, and their
total nutritional intakes, and thus BW gains, were affected. These findings are consistent with research foJournal of Dairy Science Vol. 108 No. 4, 2025

cused on accelerated milk feeding growth rates prevalent
throughout the literature, indicating that the current calf
feeding standards in Australia are inadequate (Jasper and
Weary, 2002; Borderas et al., 2009; Khan et al., 2011b).
Heifers entered the postweaning phase with a 15-kg
difference between the high and low preweaning treatments (Figure 1). Grain and pasture allocations were manipulated throughout the postweaning phase to achieve
treatment target growth trajectories. Once BW converged
in the postweaning phase (after 13 mo of age; see Figure
2.), heifers from the low preweaning treatments needed
additional grain (as seen in Table 4) to hold the same
weight as those from the high preweaning treatments,
and it became increasingly difficult to slow the growth
rate of the high preweaning groups. Postweaning treatment design was based upon the current Australian heifer
rearing strategies, with specific BW growth targets set
to achieve the same precalving BW for the postweaning
treatments. It was therefore important that the separate
postweaning treatment groups (HH with LH and HL with
LL) achieved and then maintained the same average BW
throughout the postweaning period. Although we suspect
some form of carryover effect on growth, or nutritional
intake from the preweaning phase on the high preweaning
treatment group, this cannot be confirmed, as individual
pasture intakes were not recorded; therefore we do not
have full nutritional intakes to measure such efficiencies.
Existing evidence from Holloway and Butts (1983) partially supports this premise when beef calves were reared
on various pasture types in the preweaning phase, which
influenced the pattern of postweaning growth. However,
these findings are not consistent with other studies. For
instance, Rosadiuk et al. (2021) reported no effects on

previously applied treatments in metabolic biomarkers is
supported by previous work from this group Ockenden et
al. (2023), as well as De Paula et al. (2022), Rosadiuk et
al. (2021), and (Kesser et al., 2017).
The interaction effect leading to higher serum BHB
levels in the low-treatment heifers at 6 wk of age, with no
difference between treatments afterward, was expected.
Although the higher BHB concentrations in the restricted
milk-fed heifers during the preweaning phase were anticipated, the rationale for this is equivocal in literature.
High BHB in restricted milk-fed heifers has been thought
to be due, at least in part, to increased rumen function
because of their greater concentrate intake as they try to
compensate for the limiting nutrients provided by milk
Journal of Dairy Science Vol. 108 No. 4, 2025

(Deelen et al., 2016; Byrne et al., 2017). However, this
observation is not consistent (Suarez-Mena et al., 2017),
and other studies have suggested that higher BHB may,
in fact, also indicate metabolic stress in these animals
(Schwarzkopf et al., 2019; Ockenden et al., 2023). The
increase in BHB in the postweaning phase, however, is
likely a result of an increase in rumen function and the
transition from a liquid to a solid diet at 8 and 13 mo
of age (Nemati et al., 2015). We found that NEFA did
not differ between treatments at any point and decreased
postweaning for all treatment groups. The higher NEFA
preweaning is most likely a result of the high concentration of fatty acids present in milk (Schwarzkopf et al.,
2019), and, after weaning, NEFA levels reduced.
Interactions between preweaning treatment and age
were evident for both glucose and insulin. In the preweaning phase the primary source of energy is supplied
by glucose and fatty acids in milk. Glucose levels were
highest for all treatments at 6 wk of age while consuming milk, and then reduced as they transitioned from a
monogastric animal to a ruminant and energy sources
shifted (Baldwin et al., 2004; Suarez-Mena et al., 2017).
Further, heifers on the high preweaning treatment had
significantly higher glucose levels than the low milkfed heifers in the preweaning phase (P < 0.001). Similar
treatment effects have been observed in previous experiments, where heifers consuming greater volumes of milk

This can indicate a reduction in insulin sensitivity, which
is further supported by our QUICKI index results, and
has previously been described in the postweaning phase
(Pantophlet et al., 2016; Rosadiuk et al., 2021). One of the
primary concerns around feeding high volumes of milk
in the preweaning phase is the potential for permanent
detrimental effects on glucose metabolism, specifically
insulin sensitivity (van den Borne et al., 2006; Yunta
et al., 2015; Hammon et al., 2018). When applying the
QUICKI insulin sensitivity check, interactions were also
detected between pre- and postweaning treatment with
age separately. This indicated that heifers on a greater
plane of nutrition had decreased insulin sensitivity at the
time of elevated nutrition independent of the previous
treatment, suggesting no long-term or permanent effects
of feeding a higher volume of milk.
IGF-1 was higher in the high milk-fed heifers than
the low milk-fed heifers at 6 wk of age, after which the
effects of preweaning treatment disappeared. IGF-1 has
been highly correlated with animal performance, including metabolic profiles and growth (Rodríguez-Sánchez
et al., 2015; De Paula et al., 2022). Therefore, the differences at 6 wk of age would be expected, as this reflects
nutritional status between treatments at the time. The
lack of difference at 8 mo also indicates the minimal nutritional difference between treatments at that time, while
also suggesting no carryover effect from the increased
nutrition preweaning. However, at 13 mo, when insulin
was numerically higher for the high postweaning group,
we would expect to observe a more noticeable difference
in IGF-1 levels as well (McGuire et al., 1995; De Paula
et al., 2022). This suggests that in the postweaning phase,
our nutritional inputs may not have differed enough from
each other at 13 mo of age. Pasture intake was also restricted for all heifers at the time of 13-mo sampling in
an attempt to slow the HL growth rate. This nutritional
restriction, while weight gains were still evident, may
have affected IGF-1 levels in all animals and contributed
to the lack of differences detected at 13 mo of age.
Our results do not support our second hypothesis, as
they indicate benefits of accelerated nutrition on metabolism at the time of increased nutrition only, and lack
Journal of Dairy Science Vol. 108 No. 4, 2025

3526

of carryover benefit from the preweaning phase. The
lack of preweaning metabolic imprinting in dairy heifers
is consistent with other studies looking at similar depth
of metabolic and endocrine biomarkers (Kesser et al.,
2017; Rosadiuk et al., 2021; De Paula et al., 2022). The
biomarkers investigated in this experiment are commonly used when investigating metabolic state of lactating
dairy cows during the transition period. This selection
allows for future investigation into energy balance to
determine whether early-life treatments have any influence on the resilience of the dairy cow in a metabolically
challenging time such as lactation. Kenéz et al. (2018)
were able to distinguish differences in metabolism
in first-lactation heifers reared by different preweaning nutritional strategies using metabolomic methods.
Therefore, more studies using this more intricate level
of analysis may also be necessary to fully understand
the mechanisms underlying any evidence of metabolic
developmental programming.
CONCLUSIONS

Increased levels of nutrition in the preweaning and
postweaning phases of development improved growth
rates and metabolic characteristics of heifers at the time
of increased nutrition. The observed advantages in metabolism were independent of the previous treatment,
suggesting limited signs of developmental programming
and little carryover effect from increased preweaning
nutrition in the postweaning phase. Feeding dairy heifers increased volumes of milk in the preweaning phase
reduced concentrate intakes but increased growth rates,
glucose, insulin, and IGF-1 levels while reducing levels
of BHB. In the postweaning phase, increased level of
nutrition also improved growth rates and increased insulin levels at the time of increased nutrition. Given these
results, it would appear that accelerated preweaning nutrition is important for the metabolic characteristics of a
calf but does not greatly influence the future metabolic
phenotype of the cow. Further work is needed to investigate the future reproduction and production outcomes of
these heifers.
